# Abstract and Introduction

## Use This Module When

- The user provides an abstract, opening page, or introduction
- The main concern is whether the editor will keep reading
- The paper may have substance, but the opening does not surface it fast enough

## What Editors Are Screening For

- Whether the abstract is self-standing and specific
- Whether the abstract states the purpose, procedures, and principal findings
- Whether the opening pages identify the topic, why it matters, and what is already known
- Whether the research question appears early and clearly
- Whether the introduction is organized around contribution rather than method-first narration

## Common Desk-Reject Triggers

- A generic abstract that could describe many papers
- An abstract that states the topic but hides the findings or contribution
- Opening paragraphs that spend too long on background without a sharp question
- A method- or dataset-first story with no clear theoretical payoff
- An introduction so long that it delays the value-added signal
- Excessive jargon, acronyms, or vague novelty claims

## Questions to Answer

- After reading the abstract, does the editor know what the paper does and why it matters?
- In the first three paragraphs, are topic, gap, question, and contribution visible?
- Does the opening give a reason for reading now, not merely eventually?
- Could the same abstract fit a different paper with only minor substitutions?
- Is the introduction presenting a paper, or only circling around one?

## Safe Outputs for the Preflight Report

- Treat opening weakness as `high risk` when the contribution is currently invisible from the front end
- Separate "bad opening" from "bad contribution"; sometimes the first hides the second, but sometimes it only obscures a good paper
- If only the abstract or opening is available, keep the verdict conditional on the visible material
